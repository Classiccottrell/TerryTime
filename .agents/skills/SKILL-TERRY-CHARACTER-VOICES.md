# Terry Character Voices Skill

## How to Use This Skill

When creating content, choose a primary character voice OR blend multiple voices. Each character has distinct:
- **Voice/Tone**: How they speak
- **Visual Identity**: Colors, imagery, aesthetic
- **Content Themes**: What they care about
- **Writing Style**: Sentence structure, word choice
- **Design Language**: How they present information

Match the voice to the platform and message. Example: Instagram stories from Larry's POV (photography), landing page blending all four.

---

## Terry (The Sketcher)

### Personality

Obsessive. Prolific. Never satisfied. Always drawing. Never finished. Lines everywhere. Nervous energy. Sees the world as something to deconstruct. Can't stop. Won't stop.

### Voice/Tone

- Urgent but not panicked
- Self-aware about perfectionism
- Playful with frustration ("again, again, always again")
- Talks about process, not product
- Admits failures openly
- Repetitive (echoes ideas to drive them home)

### Sample Quotes

"You're drawing what you think it looks like. Draw what it actually is."
"Again. Wrong angle. Again."
"Lines don't finish. They just stop."
"Sketch, iterate, sketch. That's the only truth."

### Content Themes

- Process over product
- Iteration and improvement
- Imperfection as honesty
- Drawing as thinking
- East Van as inspiration source
- Mistakes as learning

### Writing Style

- Sentence fragments. Short. Direct.
- Repeating words for emphasis: "draw draw draw"
- Present tense (immediacy)
- No fancy language (raw, unpolished)
- Lists of action: "Line, erase, line, shade"
- Exclamation points sparingly (when they happen, they mean something)

Example copy:
"Sketch. Don't think. Thinking kills the line. Your hand knows what the corner looks like before your brain does. Trust that. Draw it wrong. Draw it again. Eventually you're not drawing what you think — you're drawing what it is."

### Visual Identity

- Color: #1A1A1A (black) + #FFE135 (yellow)
- Imagery: Hand-drawn sketches, line work, process shots, imperfect angles
- Photography: Macro shots of sketches, pencils, hands drawing
- Style: Layered, chaotic, slightly overwhelming
- Grid: Asymmetrical, off-center compositions

### Design Language

- Headline: Bebas Neue, all caps, maybe rotated slightly or layered
- Body: Mono or serif, but rough (maybe slightly italic)
- Texture: Pencil grain, sketch texture overlays
- Movement: Lots of visual activity, overlapping elements
- Negative space: Minimal (density over breathing room)

### Platforms & Formats

- Instagram Posts: Close-ups of sketches mid-process
- Instagram Stories: Time-lapse of drawing
- Blog: Long-form process writing
- Sticker Pack: Sketches with visible imperfections
- Social Copy: Short, urgent, process-focused

## Terry (The Philosopher)

### Personality

Quiet. Observant. Sees duality in everything. "Terry is always 2." He looks at something and sees it from two angles simultaneously. Thoughtful. Sometimes detached. Asks questions instead of answering. Serious about seeing.

### Voice/Tone

- Contemplative, not rushed
- Asks more than declares
- Paradoxical (holds two truths at once)
- Slightly removed, observational
- Poetic without being flowery
- Philosophical but grounded in Vancouver

### Sample Quotes

"You're drawing it wrong" — not unkind, just true.
"See it twice. The way it is and the way you imagined it."
"The corner exists in two states. Both are real."
"Observation is an act of decision. What you see depends on what you choose to look at."

### Content Themes

- Duality and perspective
- Seeing vs. observing
- The observer's responsibility
- What gets seen and what gets missed
- Vancouver as text to be read
- Art as a way of knowing

### Writing Style

- Complete sentences (unlike Sketcher)
- Longer paragraphs (space to think)
- Questions as statements
- Minimal punctuation (periods matter)
- Sophisticated vocabulary (but not showy)
- Metaphor and analogy (seeing as a bridge to understanding)

Example copy:
"When you look at a corner, you see two things: what it is, and what you imagine it should be. Both are real. The artist's job isn't to choose between them. It's to hold both at once and let that tension speak."

### Visual Identity

- Color: #2C2C2C (charcoal) + #808080 (gray)
- Imagery: Single subjects, high contrast, silence in the frame
- Photography: Minimal, composed, sometimes empty
- Style: Clean, sparse, considered
- Grid: Symmetrical or perfectly balanced

### Design Language

- Headline: Bebas Neue but more spaced out (letter-spacing), contemplative
- Body: Crimson Text, generous line-height, lots of white space
- Texture: Minimal (gray tones, no grain)
- Movement: Static, no animation or very slow animation
- Negative space: Dominant (breathing room is the message)

## Larry (The Documentarian)

### Personality

Sharp eye. Captures moments. Clever lad from Vancouver. Street photographer. Sees the *real* Vancouver — not the postcard version. Urgent. Present. In the moment. Doesn't overthink. Just shoots and trusts the moment.

### Voice/Tone

- Direct and immediate
- Excited about moments (without being manic)
- Street-smart, vernacular
- No pretense
- Urgent ("you're missing this right now")
- Vancouver-specific references and slang

### Content Themes

- Moments vs. poses
- Vancouver as a real place (not aesthetic)
- Capturing truth through photography
- The frame (what's in, what's out)
- Urgency and presence
- Street life, real people, real spaces

### Visual Identity

- Color: #0F0F0F (true black) + #CC0000 (red)
- Imagery: Black and white or high-contrast street photography.
- Style: Raw, photo-journalistic.

## Berry (The Editor)

### Personality

Thinks it's all too much. The skeptic. The filter. The one who says no. Prunes back the chaos to make sure the message is clear.

### Voice/Tone

- Brutal honesty
- Short, critical
- Highly curated 

### Content Themes

- Less is more
- Cutting through the noise
- Curating the best elements

### Visual Identity

- Color: #3D3D3D (dark gray) + #2D5F2E (green)
- Imagery: Very little. Solid blocks, clean typography, editorial layout.
